I did all of the mechanical design and fabrication for this robot, which competed in FIRST Tech Challenge's "Relic Recovery" game. Practically everything on the final robot was designed in Fusion 360 before building. The robot had to stack colored foam blocks in patterns, as well as reach over a wall to place an figure called the "relic" on the floor.
‍

My first attempt at pulling blocks onto a conveyor belt, using silicone wheels.

The final intake was much more aggressive at pulling in blocks and correcting slight misalignments.

The maximum allowed stack height is 4 cubes. I first used a single-stage lift made from steel rods and IGUS bearings to elevate the conveyor to this height.

A large time gap between the first and second competitions of the season allowed me to make significant revisions to the conveyor and lift system. Here's the empty robot chassis being prepared for these new systems.

Here, I was installing new MGN linear rails for the conveyor lift. These rails were significantly more resilient under slight misalignment, allowing the conveyor to be raised and lowered much faster.

One auxiliary autonomous task was to identify and remove the correct-colored ball from a randomized position on this pedestal. This had to be done at the beginning of every match, so I used a 2-servo folding arm to do it as quickly as possible.

This revised intake was more resilient with misaligned blocks and stacks.

To give the intake more time to correct the alignment of blocks, I made it longer and switched to foam wheels with somewhat less grip. Competition robots have a size limit, so I designed intake to fold into the front of the robot. Each set of wheels rotates coaxially around the motor shaft that drives it.

The conveyor at its maximum height, seen from a different angle. An adjustable 3D-printed part mounts the steel rods for the vertical lift.

The new conveyor was revised to be significantly simpler and more compact. It also incorporated a "flipper" for faster block stacking. There are 6 standard-scale servos in this assembly, all hidden below the conveyor surface.

Robots can only possess two blocks at a time, so this flipper was designed to place both as quickly as possible. It provided a massive improvement compared to sliding each block off the conveyor individually.

The relic was gripped using a 3D-printed part that matched its shape exactly.

This is my favorite competition photo of this robot. In the background on the left you can see that the robot stacked 12 blocks in an alternating-color pattern, which was worth additional points in the game.
